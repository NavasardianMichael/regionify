Every time I try to map a regional spreadsheet, I lose thirty minutes to the same problem.

The problem isn't the map. Every mapping tool on the market can draw a choropleth. The problem is that my data column says `Île-de-France`, and the tool's canonical list says `Ile-de-France`, and the tool refuses to bind them. Or the data says `IDF`. Or `Paris Region`. Or, once memorably, `Îlle-de-France` with a typo that Excel had helpfully preserved through three exports.

Multiply that by seven-language regional taxonomies, three countries with contested internal boundaries, and clients who paste from Excel files that Excel itself has autocorrected — and you have a problem that costs an hour every time you touch it.

I built [Regionify](https://regionify.pro/?utm_source=medium&utm_medium=article&utm_campaign=intro-article) to solve exactly that problem. Here's a tour of what it does and how it works.

### What Regionify is, in one sentence

It's a browser-based tool that turns region-level data (populations, sales, election results, anything with a region column) into styled, publishable maps — without code, GIS software, or manual name-matching.

### The four things it actually does

**1. Pick a map.** 200+ ready-made country maps with real administrative divisions — provinces in Spain, régions in France, Länder in Germany, cantons in Switzerland, states in the USA, prefectures in Japan. Not just national outlines. The full list is on the homepage.

**2. Paste your data.** Excel, CSV, Google Sheets — pick whichever you're currently living in. An AI parser handles the region-name mess:

- A deterministic fuzzy matcher first handles ~80% of inputs: `Île-de-France`, `Ile-de-France`, `ile de france`, `IDF` all resolve to the same shape via Unicode normalisation, case folding, and Damerau-Levenshtein distance.
- For the remainder, a small structured LLM call maps unmatched names against the canonical region list for that country. Every AI-suggested binding surfaces in a human confirmation UI before any data is bound — no silent hallucinations into your published map.

### Why not just use Datawrapper / Flourish / D3?

Fair question. I evaluated all of them before writing a line of code, and the honest breakdown is:

- **Datawrapper** — excellent tool, I still use it for time-series charts. But the region-name matching is strict, and their watermark-free tier starts at $599/month.
- **Flourish** — beautiful outputs. Same strict-name-matching problem. Same pricing curve.
- **Mapbox Studio** — overpowered. You spend an hour learning tilesets to answer a five-minute question.
- **D3.js** — perfect for one-of-a-kind bespoke visuals, wrong tool for shipping six similar regional maps in a week.
- **QGIS** — the right tool for someone with GIS training, wrong tool for anyone else.

None of them fit the specific job of _"I have a spreadsheet, I want a styled regional map, I have five minutes."_ So I built one.

### Under the hood (for the curious)

Stack is deliberately boring:

- React 19 + TypeScript + Vite on the client
- Ant Design 6 + Tailwind v4 for UI
- Zustand for state
- Express 5 + Prisma + PostgreSQL + Redis on the server
- Deployed on a plain VPS with Docker Compose + Nginx — no Vercel, no Netlify. Full control over the SSR rendering of public embed pages was worth avoiding a serverless platform.

The AI region-parser is the one part that isn't boring, and it deserves its own article. I'll write that one next.
